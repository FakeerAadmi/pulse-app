Data privacy

Pulse never transmits biometric data. All wearable data is read locally and stored only in your browser's localStorage — the same place your intake logs live.

When you connect Apple Health or Health Connect, Pulse requests read-only permission. It cannot write to your health record. You can revoke access at any time from your device's Health app settings.

No biometric data is ever sent to any server, analytics platform, or third party — not even anonymised. The model runs entirely on-device.

Nicotine Guide
What nicotine actually does

When nicotine enters your bloodstream it reaches your brain in about 10 seconds and triggers a burst of dopamine — the chemical behind feelings of reward and calm. That sensation is real, but it fades fast, usually within 20–30 minutes, which is why the urge to use again arrives so quickly.

The more often you use, the more your brain adjusts to expect nicotine. Over time it produces less dopamine on its own, so you need nicotine just to feel normal rather than to feel good. That's why stopping is hard — the restlessness and irritability aren't weakness, they're your brain recalibrating.

What health bodies say

There is no safe level of tobacco use. The World Health Organization recommends stopping entirely as the only fully protective measure — not reducing, not switching, stopping.

For people who want to stop, the WHO recommends nicotine replacement products — gum, patch, lozenge — as a proven first step. Using a slow-release patch alongside a fast-acting gum works better than either alone, and behavioural support alongside NRT roughly doubles success rates.

Withdrawal symptoms are temporary. Most physical discomfort peaks at 48–72 hours and largely resolves within 2–4 weeks. The mental habit takes longer, but is learnable.

Sources: WHO FCTC Guidelines 2021 · Cochrane Review on NRT · NHS Stop Smoking

By product
Cigarette

A cigarette contains 10–14 mg of nicotine but you only absorb about 1–2 mg per smoke. The real harm isn't nicotine — it's the 7,000+ chemicals produced by burning tobacco, including carbon monoxide (which starves blood of oxygen) and dozens of cancer-causing compounds.

They're also the hardest to stop. Nicotine hits the brain fast, and smoking becomes tied to strong daily routines — morning coffee, after meals, stress breaks — which are their own challenge to break.

Vape / E-cigarette

Vaping heats liquid to vapour without burning anything — no tar, no carbon monoxide, no combustion byproducts. NHS and public health evidence suggests it's significantly less harmful than smoking for existing users. It's not risk-free: long-term effects are still being studied, and high-strength pods build dependency very quickly.

Each pod typically delivers 0.5–3 mg of absorbed nicotine depending on the strength and your session length.

Pouch / Snus

Pouches sit between the lip and gum and slowly release nicotine over 20–40 minutes. Nothing is burned, nothing is inhaled — making them one of the least harmful ways to use nicotine. They still create dependency, and very long-term oral health evidence is still building.

Gum / NRT

Available in 2 mg and 4 mg doses. Chew until you feel a tingle, then tuck it between cheek and gum — chewing constantly wastes the nicotine. One piece delivers 1–2 mg over about 20–30 minutes.

WHO-endorsed and licensed by the FDA and NHS as a cessation aid. Studies show NRT roughly doubles the odds of successfully stopping compared to going cold turkey.

Cigar

A single cigar contains 100–400 mg of nicotine. Even without inhaling you absorb 3–6 mg through your mouth lining. Regular use is associated with cancers of the mouth, throat, and oesophagus, and elevated heart disease risk. The "not inhaled" distinction reduces lung cancer risk — it doesn't make cigars safe.

Pipe

A typical pipe session delivers 1–3 mg of absorbed nicotine. The slower burn means less lung damage than cigarettes, but pipe tobacco still carries meaningful risk of mouth, throat, and lip cancers. It's commonly perceived as a gentler form of tobacco — the health evidence doesn't support that.

Harm comparison
Highest harm: Cigarettes, pipe, cigar — all involve burning
Moderate: Vaping — no burning, long-term effects still being studied
Lowest harm: Pouches, gum, patch — nothing burned, nothing inhaled

Lower harm doesn't mean zero risk. Stopping entirely is always the safest option. — Royal College of Physicians, 2016

Medical Terms
Nicotine Saturation
Shown as a percentage (0–100%). It represents how much nicotine is currently active in your bloodstream relative to your personal peak. At 100% you feel fully settled; below ~20% the brain begins sending craving signals — that restless, irritable edge you feel is this drop, not a 'need'.
Clear In
An estimate of how long until nicotine in your system drops to a near-zero baseline (~0.05 mg). Calculated from your current saturation using nicotine's ~2-hour half-life. Useful for planning — if you have a long meeting or flight, you can see exactly when a craving window opens.
Half-Life
The time it takes for nicotine concentration in the blood to halve. Average is ~2 hours (120 minutes), though it varies with age, liver enzyme activity, and genetics. Pulse uses this to draw the decay curve on your graph.
HRV — Heart Rate Variability
The millisecond variation between consecutive heartbeats. Higher HRV = more relaxed nervous system and better recovery. Nicotine acutely raises heart rate and suppresses HRV for 20–60 minutes after use. Chronic use blunts baseline HRV. Connected devices (Apple Health, Garmin) provide real readings; otherwise Pulse estimates from your profile.
RHR — Resting Heart Rate
Your heart rate when fully at rest, measured in beats per minute (bpm). A healthy adult range is 50–80 bpm. Each nicotine dose temporarily raises RHR by 10–20 bpm due to adrenaline release. Chronically elevated RHR is a cardiovascular risk marker.
SpO₂ — Blood Oxygen
The percentage of haemoglobin in your blood carrying oxygen. Normal is 97–100%. Combustible products (cigarettes, cigars) introduce carbon monoxide which binds to haemoglobin instead of oxygen, dropping SpO₂ by 1–3%. Non-combustibles have minimal effect on SpO₂.
Dopamine State
A qualitative label (Baseline → Rising → Peak → Falling → Depleted) that reflects where you are in the nicotine-dopamine cycle. Nicotine triggers a dopamine release; as it fades you may feel below-baseline ('Depleted') — this is the craving mechanism, not a permanent low mood.
Stress Index
A 0–100 score derived from HRV and RHR. Below 30 = low stress, 30–60 = moderate, above 60 = elevated. Nicotine paradoxically raises this score despite feeling calming — the relief you feel is the drug reversing its own withdrawal, not genuine relaxation.
CO Clearance
Carbon monoxide from combustible products binds haemoglobin with 250× the affinity of oxygen. It takes ~5–8 hours of clean air to clear half of the CO load. Pulse tracks this separately from nicotine clearance since CO lingers far longer in the body.
Respiratory Rate
Breaths per minute at rest. Normal adult range is 12–20. Shown in the biometric strip; elevated rate can indicate stress, cardiovascular strain, or recent aerobic activity. Nicotine use acutely increases respiratory rate.
Clean Streak
The number of consecutive days without logging any nicotine intake. Streaks are motivational, not medical — but research shows cessation success rates improve sharply after 7, 14, and 30-day milestones.
Sleep Impact Score
A 0–100 score estimating how nicotine use affected last night's sleep. Late-night intake (after 9 PM) suppresses REM sleep and increases light-sleep fragmentation. Nicotine withdrawal can also disrupt sleep independently of the drug itself.

Glossary

Nicotine saturation

A percentage showing how much nicotine is currently active in your body. When it's high (above 60%), you feel settled. As it drops toward 0%, your body starts sending craving signals — that restless, reaching feeling. It takes about 2 hours for a cigarette's worth to fall by half.

Clear in

How long until your nicotine level drops to near-zero. This is the point where cravings are most likely to peak. If you're trying to space out your intakes, this number tells you how much of a window you have.

HRV (Heart Rate Variability)

A measure of how varied the gaps between your heartbeats are. Higher HRV = more relaxed and recovered nervous system. Nicotine temporarily lowers it, meaning your body is under slightly more stress even if you don't feel it. The best time to check it is first thing in the morning.

Dopamine state

An estimate of where your mood chemistry is right now. "Elevated" means nicotine is actively stimulating dopamine release. "Falling" means it's wearing off and you may start to feel a dip. "Base" means you're back to your natural baseline — which, with regular use, is often lower than it would be for a non-user.

RHR (Resting Heart Rate)

Your heart rate when you're calm and not exercising. Nicotine raises it temporarily. A lower resting HR over time generally means better cardiovascular health.

SpO₂ (Blood Oxygen)

The percentage of oxygen your blood is carrying. Normal is 95–100%. Smoking combustible products can bring this down slightly because carbon monoxide competes with oxygen in your blood.

CO Clearance

Carbon monoxide is a gas produced when tobacco burns. It's absorbed into your blood and crowds out oxygen. After your last cigarette, cigar, or pipe, it takes roughly 4–6 hours for CO to drop significantly. This card only appears for combustible products — vapes and pouches don't produce CO.

Stress index

Derived from your HRV — a rough gauge of how much your nervous system is under load. 0 = very calm, 100 = high strain. Nicotine raises this number in the short term, even though the intake may feel calming.

Clean streak

How long since your last logged intake. The ring fills as you approach 24 hours. At key milestones (20 min, 12 hours, 72 hours) the app will surface what's changing in your body — these are real, well-studied recovery markers.

Respiratory rate

How many breaths per minute. Normal is around 12–18. This is estimated based on your current state — a real wearable will give you a more accurate reading.

Sleep impact

Nicotine is a stimulant. Late-night intake (after 9 PM) tends to reduce the quality of deep sleep even if you fall asleep normally. The sleep score here reflects this — it's an estimate, not a clinical reading.
